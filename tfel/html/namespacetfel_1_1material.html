◆ OrthotropicAxesConvention

enum tfel::material::OrthotropicAxesConvention
strong

Most finite element solver can't have a uniq definition of the orthotropic axes for all the modelling hypotheses. One thus need to define a convention. This leads to many troubles when trying to define an the hill tensor or the thermal expansion.

For example, one can define a pipe using the following axes definition:

  • \(\left(rr,zz,tt,...\right)\) in \(3D\), \(2D\) axysymmetric, \(1D\) axisymmetric generalised plane strain or generalised plane stress.
  • \(\left(rr,tt,zz,...\right)\) in \(2D\) plane stress, strain, generalized plane strain.

This means that we have to exchange the second and third axis when changing from \(3D\) to \(2D\) plane strain. This is the PIPE (orthotropic axes) convention.

The various classes and functions that take an OrthotropicAxesConvention value as template parameter will take the values of the material properties in the material frame corresponding to the \(3D\) case and internally make the appropriate swaps when necessary.

Enumerator
DEFAULT 

The DEFAULT orthotropic convention does not differentiate modelling hypotheses, which is not physically significant.

PIPE 

Orthotropic axes conventions for pipes

The PIPE orthotropic convention assumes that:

  • in \(3D\), \(2D\) axysymmetric, \(1D\) axisymmetric generalised plane strain or generalised plane stress:
    • The first material axis is given by the radius of the pipe.
    • The second material axis is given by the axis of the pipe.

  • in \(2D\) plane stress, strain, generalized plane strain:

    • The first material axis is given by the radius of the pipe.
    • The second material axis is perpendicual to the radius of the pipe and to the axis of the pipe.

    In other words, the second and third axes are exchanged compared to the (3D) case.

PLATE 

Orthotropic axes conventions for plates

The PLATE orthotropic convention implies that:

  • The first material axis is the rolling direction.
  • The second material axis is the tranverse direction.
  • The third material axis is the normal direction.

The PLATE orthotropic convention can only be used in:

  • \(3D\) modelling hypothesis.
  • \(2D\) plane stress, strain and generalized plane strain modelling hypotheses.

This function computes the Barlat yield stress. The Barlat yield stress is defined by:

\[ \phi\left(\underline{\bf s}\right) = \sqrt[a]{ \frac{1}{4}\left( \sum_{i=0}^{3} \sum_{j=0}^{3} \left|s'_{i}-s''_{j}\right|^{a} \right) } \]

where \(s'_{i}\) and \(s''_{j}\) are the eigenvalues computed from two tensors \(\underline{\bf s}'\) and \(\underline{\bf s}''\) resulting from a linear transformation of the stress tensor:

\[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} \underline{\bf s}' &= \underline{\underline{L}}' \,\colon\,\underline{\bf s} \\ \underline{\bf s}'' &= \underline{\underline{L}}''\,\colon\,\underline{\bf s} \\ \end{aligned} \right. \]

.

The linear transformations \(\underline{\underline{L}}'\) and \(\underline{\underline{L}}''\) can be deduced from linear transformations \(\underline{\underline{C}}'\) and \(\underline{\underline{C}}''\). on the deviator of the stress as follows:

\[ \begin{aligned} \underline{L}' &=\underline{\underline{C}}'\,\colon\,\underline{\underline{M}} \\ \underline{L}'' &=\underline{\underline{C}}'\,\colon\,\underline{\underline{M}} \\ \end{aligned} \]

where \(\underline{\underline{M}}\) is the transformation of the stress to its deviator:

\[ \underline{\underline{M}}=\underline{\underline{I}}-\frac{1}{3}\underline{I}\,\otimes\,\underline{I} \]

The linear transformations \(\underline{\underline{C}}'\) and \(\underline{\underline{C}}''\) are defined by \(9\) coefficients (each) which describe the material orthotropy, as follows:

\[ \underline{\underline{C}}'= \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -c'_{12} & -c'_{13} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -c'_{21} & 0 & -c'_{23} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -c'_{31} & -c'_{32} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & c'_{44} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & c'_{55} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & c'_{66} \\ \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad \underline{\underline{C}}''= \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -c''_{12} & -c''_{13} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -c''_{21} & 0 & -c''_{23} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -c''_{31} & -c''_{32} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & c''_{44} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & c''_{55} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & c''_{66} \\ \end{pmatrix} \]

the derivative of the determinant of the deviator of symmetric tensor.

Let \(\underline{s}\) be a symmetric tensor and \(J_{3}\) be the determinant of \(\underline{s}'\) the deviator of \(\underline{s}\):

\[ J_{3} = \mathrm{det}\left(\underline{s}'\right) = \mathrm{det}\left(\underline{s}-\mathrm{tr}\left(\underline{s}'\right)\,\underline{I}\right) \]

This function computes \(\displaystyle\frac{\partial J_{3}}{\partial \underline{\sigma}}\).

Parameters
[in]stensor
Note
this function is a simple wrapper around the tfel::math::computeDeviatorDeterminantDerivative function.

This function computes the RambergOsgood paramters K and n which allow the approximation of the traction curve by the relation: [ p = K(s/E)**n ] where:

  • p is the cumulated plastic strain
  • s is the von mises stress
  • E is the Young modulus

This functions can be used with the mfront' IsotropicPlasticMisesFlow parser like this:

@LocalVariable real K_p;
@LocalVariable real n_p;
@InitLocalVariables{
using namespace tfel::material;
const real Rp02=...;
const real Rm=...;
const real Ag=...;
real K_r;

computeRambergOsgoodParameters(K_r,n_r,young,Rp02,Rm,Ag);
K_p=1./K_r;
n_p=1./n_r;
}
@FlowRule{
const real p0 = 1.e-6;
const real tmp = young*pow(K_p*(p+p0),n_p-1);
f = seq-tmp*K_p*(p+p0);
df_dseq = 1.;
df_dp = -n_p*K_p*tmp;
